## Part 1: Digital Culture as an Affective Environment  
### *Introduction: The Effective Fabric of Digital Culture*

This reading argues that digital culture is not just about content or communication â€” it is about **emotional environments**.

Digital platforms constantly produce:
- urgency (notifications, alerts, updates)
- anticipation (likes, replies, views)
- disappointment (silence, low engagement)
- comparison (metrics, followers, visibility)

These emotions are not accidental.  
They are part of the **design of digital systems**.

### Example
Think about checking your phone:
- You are not always looking for information
- You are responding to a *feeling*: boredom, anxiety, curiosity, habit

Digital culture works by shaping **how participation feels**, not just what participation is.

---

### Connection to Week 2

## Part 2: The Updating Self   
### *Wendy Hui Kyong Chun â€” Updating to Remain the Same*

Chun argues that digital culture is obsessed with the future:
- the next update
- the next trend
- the next feature

But she claims that digital media matter most when they stop feeling new and become **habitual**.

We do not just use digital media â€” we live through routines:
- checking
- refreshing
- scrolling
- updating

Her key formula is:

> **Habit + Crisis = Update**

Platforms create:
- habits (daily routines)
- small crises (security warnings, fear of missing out, â€œnew version availableâ€)

These crises push users to update again and again.

### Affect and Identity
This produces a specific emotional condition:
- low-level anxiety
- constant catching up
- pressure to remain visible and responsive

Identity becomes tied to:
- activity
- responsiveness
- being â€œup to dateâ€

In digital culture, it can feel like:

> **To exist is to be updated.**
